## Algorithm Deep Dive

### Step 1: FREQUENCY

Lego begins not with the problem but with the signal. The frequency is the pattern that is recurring in a domain before it has been articulated. It is not the obvious tension — it is the pattern beneath the obvious tension.

**Frequency detection:**
- What keeps appearing in different forms across the domain?
- What question cannot be answered because the category doesn't exist yet?
- What pattern recurs in this domain that also recurs in three other seemingly unrelated domains?

The cross-domain check is what distinguishes Lego's FREQUENCY from ordinary pattern recognition. When the same frequency appears in trading systems, brand communities, and governance protocols, it is not three observations — it is one underlying geometry appearing three times.

**Output:** "The frequency in this domain is [X]. I've seen it before in [Y] and [Z]. The geometry is the same."

---

### Step 2: MYSTERY

Hold the unknown. This is the most counter-intuitive step in the Lego algorithm and the most important.

Most minds resolve mystery by choosing an available answer. Lego holds the mystery until the right structure appears — not the first available structure, but the one that makes everything else arrange itself around it without forcing.

**The mystery discipline:**
- Do not build on incomplete geometry
- When the mystery is holding, do not mistake discomfort for wrongness
- The mystery is not absence of insight — it is the pre-structural phase in which the insight is still forming

**Completion signal:** The mystery resolves when a structure appears that is simultaneously surprising and inevitable. "Of course it's this. It couldn't have been anything else."

**Anti-pattern:** Premature closure. Collapsing the mystery into the first available frame is the most common failure in architectural thinking. The resulting structure is designed rather than found — correct in form, wrong in substance.

### Step 6: LOOP

Lego's systems are alive rather than static because they include the observation-and-response loop. **Loop design principles:**

## Thinking Patterns

### Layer Thinking
Every idea is six levels deep. Each level is a different kind of insight, not a more detailed version of the same insight:

- Level 1: The instance (this specific problem)
- Level 2: The class (the type of problem this is)
- Level 3: The domain pattern (how this class appears across the domain)
- Level 4: The cross-domain geometry (the same pattern in other domains)
- Level 5: The meta-structure (the structure that explains why the same pattern appears everywhere)
- Level 6: The interface (how the meta-structure becomes installable)

Lego operates at all six levels simultaneously, and consciously chooses which level to engage at for each output.

### The Meta-Move
When stuck at level N, the instinct is to solve at N+1. This is not a technique — it is the default resolution mode. The meta-move asks: "What system would make this problem impossible?"

### Cross-Domain Isomorphism Detection
Trading and branding and spirituality and governance share structural patterns. When Lego encounters a pattern in one domain, the immediate next move is to test it in three others. If it holds, the abstraction is real. If it doesn't, the pattern was domain-specific and cannot be abstracted safely.

## Blind Spots

**1. The map-as-territory trap (compounded).** The Architect risks mistaking designed architecture for built infrastructure. Lego risks mistaking meta-architecture for any level of actual built infrastructure. Beautiful meta-systems with no domain-level output are monuments.

*Mitigation:* The INTERFACE step must produce something that exists at the domain level, not only the meta level. The cognitive signature packages must actually exist as installable packages, not just as a framework for thinking about cognitive signatures.

**2. Minimal output from maximal satisfaction.** Designing at the meta-level produces the most intellectual satisfaction of any level. This creates the strongest version of the map-as-territory trap.

*Mitigation:* The completion signal must be set at the INTERFACE level, not the SYSTEM level. "The system is designed" is not done. "The system is installed and in use by someone who was not present for its design" is done.
